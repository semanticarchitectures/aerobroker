What the broker actually does
  1. Elicit requirements through a structured dialogue (asset, scope, frequency, regulatory needs, deliverables, budget, timeline).
  2. Translate business intent into technical requirements — resolution targets, sensors, post-processing, certifications.
  3. Match against the provider catalog, scoring each candidate and explaining trade-offs in plain English.
  4. Assemble a job — sometimes a single full-stack provider, sometimes a collector + processor split, sometimes 2 collectors + 1 processor.
  5. Quote with line-item pricing, alternatives, and the assumptions the quote is conditional on.
Core entities
  • Provider — capabilities, certifications, geography, pricing model & range, service modes (collection / processing / full-stack).
  • RequirementSpec — the structured spec the broker is filling in.
  • JobAssembly — roles (collection, processing, certification, ground crew), assigned provider per role, totals, alternatives, risks.
  • Quote — versioned snapshot of an assembly with line items, valid-through, T&Cs.
Why a broker (not a marketplace)

Marketplaces work when the consumer already knows what they need. Drone services consumers almost never do — they know the outcome they want (e.g. "annual structural assessment of these 40 bridges with engineer-stamped reports"), and the broker's value is translating that into a procurable spec, then orchestrating providers. The conversation IS the product.

Open questions worth chewing on
  • Who holds liability when output fails QA — broker, collector, or processor?
  • How is provider capacity reserved during the quote window?
  • What's the data handoff format between collector and processor, and who owns it?
  • How are recurring jobs re-bid — annually, or locked for the term?
  • Does the broker take a fixed % cut, or value-priced on savings vs. baseline?
